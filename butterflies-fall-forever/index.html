butterflies fall forever

subtext in the music of he is we | 14 feb 2015

introduction

I first came across he is we's music in the form of their Old Demos, which i found while browsing bandcamp a few years back. It was a touch on the expensive side ($5, lol) but people's opinions seemed positive and after listening to a few tracks i figured hey, why not. It filled a genre niche (lovingly referred to as shitty pop) missing in my music library, and the songs were genuinely enjoyable to listen to. This was 2012, at the start of my freshman year of college.

In 2013, i picked up their ep, Skip to the Good Part, and honestly it took me a while to appreciate it. Aaron gillespie's performance in the opening track threw me off (still does; one of the strengths of he is we's music is its one-sided nature), and the tracks as a whole had a polished slightly over-the top sound i wasn't really used to. I liked Skip to the Good Part, though, and eventually the album grew on me as a whole.

At the end of 2014, i finally purchased My Forever, for the most part completing my collection. And, like Skip to the Good Part, many of the songs threw me for a loop the first time around, but gradually grew on me as time went on and i gained a greater understanding of them.

Now, he is we's songs can be very problematic. This is something i noticed from the beginning, and at first i wrote it off with well, it's shitty pop; shitty pop is allowed to be problematic. But that's not really true; firstly, shitty pop isn't just "allowed" to be problematic, it's as political and influential as the rest of it, maybe more so; secondly, rather than subtracting from the experience, the problematic nature of the tracks actually made me like them more. This was something that had to be reconciled: What is it about this music that makes its problematic messages add to the experience? How can that be a good thing?

This line of inquiry is what eventually led me down the rabbit-hole into these songs' deeper meaning. Or, i should say, my deeper interpretation of them. For another strength of this music is that it will invariably mean something different to every person who hears it, and my connecting-of-the-dots is no more valid or justified than anyone else's. But i hope that by analysing my own interpretations, we can maybe uncover something about messages, media, and methods of storytelling as a whole.
